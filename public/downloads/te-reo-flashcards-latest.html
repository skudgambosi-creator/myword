Directional particles
These four particles attach to verbs to show direction relative to the speaker. They are one of the most expressive features of te reo — haere (go), haere mai (come towards me), haere atu (go away from me), haere ake (go up), haere iho (go down). Whakarongo mai means listen towards me — i.e. listen here!

These small words do enormous structural work. Mastering them is the key to sentence construction. Te reo is VSO (verb-subject-object) — the particle always opens the sentence and signals what kind of sentence it is.

Sentence structure
Te reo is VSO — Verb, Subject, Object. English says "I eat food". Te reo says "Eating, I, food". The verb always comes first. Every sentence opens with a particle that acts as a mode selector, telling you what kind of sentence it is before anything else.
The six core particles
ParticleUseExample
kei tepresent actionkei te kai au — I am eating
isimple pasti kai au — I ate
kanarrative / sequenceka haere ia — she went
e...anaemphatic present (right now)e kai ana au — I am eating right now
koidentity — specific thingko Mere tōku ingoa — my name is Mere
hedescription — a/an categoryhe kaiako au — I am a teacher
Ko vs He — the common confusion
Ko spotlights a specific identity. He places something in a general category. Ko Mere tōku ingoa = my name IS Mere (that specific name). He kaiako ia = she is A teacher (one of many). Ko = spotlight. He = category.
Negatives
PositiveNegative

he kaiako auehara au i te kaiako

Pattern for kei te negation: kāore + subject + i te + verb. Pattern for he/ko negation: ehara + subject + i te/i a + thing.

Colours in te reo often have natural world origins — kākāriki is both green and a native parakeet, kōwhai is both yellow and the native flowering tree, karaka is both orange and a native tree with orange berries. The language and the land are deeply connected.

Possessives
Te reo has two possession classes — ō and ā. Ō class is for things you don't control or are subordinate to: family, body parts, feelings, your home, your land. Ā class is for things you control or are active over: objects you make or use, actions, food you eat. Practical shortcut: tō- for people and places, tā- for things you do or make. Not perfectly accurate but covers 90% of everyday use.
Te ReoEnglishClass
tōkumyō — people, places, feelings
tōuyour (one person)ō
tō māuaour (two, not you)ō
tō tāuaour (two, incl. you)ō
tō mātouour (three+, not you)ō
tō tātouour (all)ō
tākumyā — actions, objects
tāuyour (one person)ā
tā māuaour (two, not you)ā
tā tāuaour (two, incl. you)ā
tā mātouour (three+, not you)ā
tā tātouour (all)ā
Examples from our sentences: ko ___ tōku ingoa (my name — ō class because you don't control your name / identity) vs he aha tāu mahi (your work — ā class because work is something you do actively).
